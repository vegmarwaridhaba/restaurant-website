Frequently Asked Questions

Everything you need to know about Fixed Deposits

What is a Fixed Deposit (FD)? ▼
A Fixed Deposit is a financial instrument offered by banks where you deposit a lump sum for a fixed period at a predetermined interest rate. Unlike savings accounts, FDs offer higher, guaranteed returns. The interest rate is locked in at the time of deposit and doesn't change with market conditions, making FDs a safe, predictable investment choice.
How is FD interest calculated? ▼
FD interest uses compound interest formula: A = P × (1 + r/n)^(n×t), where P is principal, r is annual rate, n is compounding frequency per year, and t is time in years. The more frequent the compounding, the higher the effective return. Monthly compounding gives slightly better results than quarterly, which beats half-yearly and annual compounding.
Which compounding frequency is best for FD? ▼
Monthly compounding yields the highest returns since interest is added to principal 12 times a year, which itself earns interest. Quarterly compounding (used by most banks in India) is the most common. Half-yearly and annual compounding give lower returns at the same stated rate. When comparing FDs across banks, always compare effective annual yield, not just the stated rate.
Is FD interest taxable in India? ▼
Yes, FD interest is fully taxable as per your income tax slab. If total FD interest in a financial year exceeds ₹40,000 (₹50,000 for senior citizens), the bank deducts 10% TDS. You can avoid TDS by submitting Form 15G/15H if your total income is below the taxable limit. The maturity amount shown in this calculator is pre-tax.
Can I withdraw FD before maturity? ▼
Yes, most banks allow premature FD withdrawal, but a penalty of 0.5%–1% is typically deducted from the applicable rate. Some banks also offer "flexi FDs" or sweep-in accounts that allow partial withdrawal without penalty. It's important to check premature withdrawal terms before booking a long-tenure FD.

Understanding FD Returns

Fixed Deposits remain one of the safest investment instruments in India. While returns may not match equity markets, the guaranteed, risk-free nature makes FDs ideal for capital protection and short-to-medium term goals. This calculator helps you compare scenarios — different tenures, rates and compounding frequencies — before you commit.

FD vs Other Investments

While FDs offer safety and guaranteed returns, mutual funds and equity investments may offer higher returns over long periods but with more risk. For emergency funds, short-term goals (1–3 years) and risk-averse investors, FDs remain an excellent choice. Smart FD Insights

Things that genuinely affect your returns

⚡
Higher compounding = higher returns
Monthly compounding earns more than annual. The same rate gives different results depending on how often interest is added to principal.
📅
Longer tenure boosts interest exponentially
Compounding rewards patience. Doubling the tenure doesn't just double interest — it grows much faster due to interest-on-interest effect.
📊
Small rate differences matter over time
A 0.5% rate difference may seem small, but over 5–10 years on a large deposit, the gap in returns becomes significant — often thousands of rupees.

Frequently Asked Questions

Everything you need to know about Fixed Deposits

What is a Fixed Deposit (FD)? ▼
A Fixed Deposit is a financial instrument offered by banks where you deposit a lump sum for a fixed period at a predetermined interest rate. Unlike savings accounts, FDs offer higher, guaranteed returns. The interest rate is locked in at the time of deposit and doesn't change with market conditions, making FDs a safe, predictable investment choice.
How is FD interest calculated? ▼
FD interest uses compound interest formula: A = P × (1 + r/n)^(n×t), where P is principal, r is annual rate, n is compounding frequency per year, and t is time in years. The more frequent the compounding, the higher the effective return. Monthly compounding gives slightly better results than quarterly, which beats half-yearly and annual compounding.
Which compounding frequency is best for FD? ▼
Monthly compounding yields the highest returns since interest is added to principal 12 times a year, which itself earns interest. Quarterly compounding (used by most banks in India) is the most common. Half-yearly and annual compounding give lower returns at the same stated rate. When comparing FDs across banks, always compare effective annual yield, not just the stated rate.
Is FD interest taxable in India? ▼
Yes, FD interest is fully taxable as per your income tax slab. If total FD interest in a financial year exceeds ₹40,000 (₹50,000 for senior citizens), the bank deducts 10% TDS. You can avoid TDS by submitting Form 15G/15H if your total income is below the taxable limit. The maturity amount shown in this calculator is pre-tax.
Can I withdraw FD before maturity? ▼
Yes, most banks allow premature FD withdrawal, but a penalty of 0.5%–1% is typically deducted from the applicable rate. Some banks also offer "flexi FDs" or sweep-in accounts that allow partial withdrawal without penalty. It's important to check premature withdrawal terms before booking a long-tenure FD.

Understanding FD Returns

Fixed Deposits remain one of the safest investment instruments in India. While returns may not match equity markets, the guaranteed, risk-free nature makes FDs ideal for capital protection and short-to-medium term goals. This calculator helps you compare scenarios — different tenures, rates and compounding frequencies — before you commit.

FD vs Other Investments

While FDs offer safety and guaranteed returns, mutual funds and equity investments may offer higher returns over long periods but with more risk. For emergency funds, short-term goals (1–3 years) and risk-averse investors, FDs remain an excellent choice. Use this calculator alongside the RD and savings interest calculators to compare all options.
